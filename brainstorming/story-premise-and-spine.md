# Story premise and spine

## Purpose

Convert ranked story kernels into a chosen story proposition.

This pipeline determines:
- which kernel becomes the actual story
- who carries the story
- what makes the story original in execution
- what the story is arguing
- what question the story will answer
- what the minimal causal spine is

This pipeline should produce:
- a selected kernel
- protagonist and opposition center
- actant map
- private-world conflict map
- concept line
- premise line
- designing principle
- central dramatic question
- plot-family classification
- scale classification
- story spine

This document is intentionally standalone and repeats repository assumptions on purpose.

---

## Core Rule

A kernel becomes a story only when incompatible private worlds are forced into collision.

Do not choose the “biggest” kernel.
Choose the kernel whose pressures, actors, and unfolding process create the strongest world-specific causality.

---

## Design Goal

Move from event seed to story-bearing proposition.

A strong story proposition should answer:
- why this story belongs to this world
- why this character must be the center
- what kind of reading experience the story offers
- what irreversible conflict the story is really about
- how the story’s process differs from a generic sequence of scenes

---

## Definitions

### Concept
The compressed statement of what makes the story interesting.

It should answer:
- who is under pressure
- what is changing
- why this is not generic
- what kind of experience this offers

### Premise
The story’s argumentative line.

It is not mere setup.
It states:
- the kind of person or social position at the center
- the conflict pressure
- the likely direction of consequence or transformation

### Designing Principle
The story’s unique unfolding process.

This is what makes the story original in execution, not merely in topic.

### Central Dramatic Question
A concrete yes/no or either/or question that the climax must answer.

### Story Spine
The shortest causal throughline of the story.

### Plot Family
The dominant narrative pleasure structure:
- epic
- epistemic
- dramatic
- hybrid

### Private-World Conflict Map
A map of the gap between:
- actual position
- knowledge world
- desire world
- obligation world
- threshold world

## Phase 0: Re-open the top 3 kernels

Do not commit too early.

For each finalist, ask again:
- whose life actually gets forced out of equilibrium?
- who pays first?
- who pays worst?
- what public consequence spreads outward?
- what private contradiction becomes unavoidable?
- what can this story reveal through action rather than exposition?
- does the kernel want short form or long form?
- what kind of climax does the kernel naturally aim toward?

### Rule

The strongest kernel is not always the one with the biggest stakes.

Prefer the kernel with:
- stronger causal pressure
- better character contradiction
- deeper world specificity
- cleaner dramatic question
- richer artifact or institutional leverage
- clearer possibility for irreversible choice

---

## Phase 1: Build the actant map

Assign functional roles.

Do not confuse these with trope labels.

### Required Functional Roles

- **Subject:** who is trying to accomplish something
- **Object:** what is sought, prevented, hidden, preserved, escaped, proved, or destroyed
- **Sender:** what pressure launches the action
- **Receiver:** who stands to gain or lose if the object is attained
- **Helper:** what person, institution, route, text, skill, belief, or accident aids movement
- **Opponent:** what person, institution, law, body, terrain, rumor, or clock resists movement

### Rule

A helper or opponent does not have to be a person.

It may be:
- a calendar
- a debt system
- a species anatomy fact
- an archive restriction
- a monastic rule
- a contaminated landscape
- a forged manual
- a funeral law

### Output

For each finalist, produce a compact actant map before selecting the final story.

---

## Phase 2: Build the private-world conflict map

For each major actor, map five layers.

### 1. Actual Position
What is materially true for them right now?

### 2. Knowledge World
What do they think is true?
What do they know?
What are they wrong about?

### 3. Desire World
What future do they want?

### 4. Obligation World
What do they owe, fear, promise, or legally owe?

### 5. Threshold World
What line will they not cross until forced?

### Required Characters

At minimum, map:
- protagonist candidate
- primary opponent
- key helper
- institutional representative
- artifact-linked witness or claimant

### Rule

A strong story proposition exists only when at least two major actors live in incompatible private worlds.

---

## Phase 3: Choose the center of narration

Select:
- protagonist
- main opposing force
- likely point-of-view strategy
- dominant contradiction

### Selection Questions

- Who is forced into irreversible choice?
- Who is best positioned to reveal the world through pressure?
- Whose misreading matters most?
- Whose decision will define the climax?
- Who stands at the best intersection of private and public stakes?
- Who reveals the world naturally rather than by lecture?

### Rule

The protagonist does not have to be the most powerful actor.

The protagonist must be the actor whose decisions best convert world pressure into story movement.

---

## Phase 4: Write the concept line

Compress the selected story into one strong sentence.

### Format

person in world-specific position  
+ under world-specific pressure  
+ confronting world-specific change  
+ in a mode that implies the reading experience

### Quality Test

A strong concept line should make clear:
- who the story is about
- what is forcing movement
- why this is specific to the world
- what kind of narrative pleasure is promised

### Failure Mode

If the line is only distinctive because of proper nouns, it is still generic.

---

## Phase 5: Write the premise line

Write the story’s argumentative core.

### Format

When a person defined by contradiction or social position  
pursues or resists something under this world’s pressures,  
the pursuit produces a specific transformation, cost, exposure, or ruin.

### Rules

- The premise must imply direction.
- Do not leave it morally blank.
- It must suggest what kind of change, compromise, or destruction the story is testing.
- It must bind character to world pressure, not treat world as background flavor.

### Examples of premise pressure types

- duty vs appetite
- ritual purity vs survival
- law vs kinship
- piety vs evidence
- ambition vs bodily limit
- social belonging vs truth
- institutional loyalty vs ordinary-life harm
- secrecy vs responsibility

---

## Phase 6: Write the designing principle

State the unique process by which the story unfolds.

This is the heart of originality at the execution level.

### Ask

- Why is this not a standard line of scenes?
- What repeating process generates the turns?
- What formal engine makes the story feel singular?
- What part of the world becomes the machine of unfolding?

### High-yield process logics

- each chapter reinterprets the same event through different artifacts
- each movement crosses a different legal or ritual boundary
- each major turn comes from correcting one false text
- each attempt to solve the problem worsens a larger institution-level contradiction
- the route through the landscape is the structure
- the investigation is inseparable from contamination risk
- the romance grows only through forbidden practical cooperation
- every success raises public cost while lowering private trust

### Rule

“Then things get more intense” is not a designing principle.

Chronology alone is not a designing principle.

---

## Phase 7: Write the central dramatic question

Write one concrete question.

### Good forms

- Can X do Y before Z closes?
- Will X prove Y without becoming Z?
- Can X preserve A and B, or must one be sacrificed?
- Is the truth worth public release under current law?
- Can X return home without accepting the institution they now despise?
- Will X expose the false text that legitimizes the system, or use it?

### Rules

- It must be answerable by the climax.
- It must arise from the premise.
- It must be concrete enough to structure scenes.
- It must not collapse into a theme statement.

---

## Phase 8: Choose plot family and scale

Classify the story.

### Plot Family Options

#### Epic
Use when:
- hostile terrain
- travel
- survival
- extraction
- crossing thresholds
- large physical ordeal
- route logic

are primary.

#### Epistemic
Use when:
- mystery
- hidden cause
- disputed evidence
- archives
- testimony
- artifact interpretation
- legal or theological proof

are primary.

#### Dramatic
Use when:
- changing alliances
- intimacy
- betrayal
- kinship
- patronage
- faction loyalty
- social mask collapse

are primary.

#### Hybrid
Most strong world-based stories will be hybrid.

Recommended hybrid forms:
- epic + epistemic
- dramatic + epistemic
- epic + dramatic
- all three

### Scale Guidance

#### Short Story
- 1 dominant kernel
- 1-2 major reversals
- 1 main relationship shift
- 1 clear crisis
- 1 contained aftermath

#### Novella
- 1 dominant kernel
- 1 secondary kernel
- 2-3 reversals
- stronger institutional presence
- broader consequence wave

#### Novel / Questline
- 1 dominant kernel
- 2-3 secondary kernels
- multiple pressure fronts
- public and private consequence layers
- broad fallout

### Rule

Choose the smallest scale that can fully express the consequences.

Do not inflate form merely because the world is large.

---

## Phase 9: Write the story spine

Write the shortest causal chain.

### Spine Template

- There was once...
- Every day...
- But one day...
- Because of that...
- Because of that...
- Because of that...
- Until finally...
- And afterward...

### Rules

- Keep it causal.
- Keep it world-specific.
- Keep it irreversible.
- Keep it tied to the dramatic question.

### Failure Mode

If the spine sounds generic, the story still is.

---

## Phase 10: Cross-validate concept, premise, and spine

The selected story proposition must align across all layers.

### Validation Questions

- Does the concept line promise what the premise actually tests?
- Does the premise imply the climax?
- Does the designing principle generate the kinds of scenes the spine requires?
- Does the dramatic question arise naturally from the kernel?
- Does the plot-family choice match the actual reading pleasure?
- Does the scale choice fit the consequence load?
- Would removing the world’s institutions, embodiment, or artifacts materially weaken the proposition?

### Rejection Conditions

Reject or revise if:
- the protagonist is chosen for centrality rather than pressure
- the premise is merely descriptive
- the dramatic question is too abstract
- the designing principle is just “events happen in order”
- the spine depends on coincidence
- the story remains portable to another world with minimal changes
- the world is present only as flavor

## Acceptance Tests

This pipeline succeeds only if all of these are true.

### Proposition Tests
- the selected kernel now has a clear protagonist center
- at least two major actors live in incompatible private worlds
- the concept line is specific without requiring lore dump
- the premise implies transformation, exposure, compromise, or ruin
- the dramatic question can be answered by a climax
- the spine reads as causality, not topic summary

### World-Specificity Tests
- the proposition depends on this world’s institutions
- embodiment or material conditions matter
- artifacts or text traditions matter when claimed
- ordinary life is relevant to stakes
- history leaves residue in the proposition

### Scale and Mode Tests
- plot family matches actual pleasure
- scale matches consequence load
- the designing principle creates a distinct unfolding process

### Canon Tests
- no stealth canon creation
- no accidental mystery resolution
- no contradiction with invariants
- no local/global drift in what is treated as world truth

## Where This Pipeline Stops

- a selected kernel
- a clear center
- an actant map
- private-world conflict maps
- a concept
- a premise
- a designing principle
- a dramatic question
- a plot family
- a scale
- a spine

It does not yet build the full chronological fabula or milestone architecture.

- a kernel

- a pressure web

- a designing principle
- a spine
- a milestone architecture
