# Story architecture

## Purpose

Build the full causal architecture of a story from an approved story proposition.

This pipeline should produce:
- a chronological fabula
- kernel vs satellite beat classification
- milestone architecture
- relationship and opposition architecture
- artifact integration plan
- consequence map
- canon risk report
- continuity update list
- residue / afterimage plan
- clean handoff to scene planning

This document is intentionally standalone and repeats repository assumptions on purpose.

---

## Core Rule

Architecture is not a template painted over a premise.

Architecture is the causal arrangement of consequential events emerging from:
- world state
- character contradiction
- institutional response
- artifact leverage
- geography
- embodiment
- time pressure
- aftermath

Every major beat must change at least one of:
- access
- knowledge
- alliance
- status
- territory
- bodily condition
- law position
- resource position
- ritual status
- time pressure

If a beat changes none of these, it is probably ornamental.

---

## Design Goal

End with a complete story architecture such that prose scenes are the only missing layer.

A strong architecture should:
- preserve world specificity
- build from loaded normality into irreversible change
- make the world react
- force crisis through incompatible values
- let climax answer the dramatic question
- show fallout beyond interior feeling
- return something to the world in the form of residue

---

## Definitions

### Fabula
The chronological chain of events in actual story order, regardless of how the story will later be presented.

### Kernel Beat
A beat whose removal changes the causal structure of the story.

### Satellite Beat
A beat whose removal changes emphasis, texture, or pacing but not the core causal chain.

### Milestone
A major structural turning function in the story.

### Consequence Wave
A cluster of outward reactions following a breach or decision.

### Residue
A durable trace the story leaves in the world:
- a rumor
- a decree
- a scar
- a local custom
- a censored text
- a mythic misremembering
- a new artifact

## Phase 0: Build the chronological fabula first

Before arranging dramatic presentation, write the actual chain of events in chronological order.

### Include

- pre-story conditions
- triggering event
- first attempts
- first consequences
- institutional reactions
- new discoveries
- escalations
- crisis conditions
- decisive act
- fallout
- altered equilibrium

### Mark each beat as either:

- **Kernel Beat**
- **Satellite Beat**

### Rule

Every kernel beat must change at least one of:
- access
- knowledge
- alliance
- status
- territory
- bodily condition
- law position
- resource position
- ritual status
- time pressure

If a beat changes none of these, cut it, merge it, or downgrade it.

---

## Phase 1: Stress-test causality

Before building milestone structure, interrogate the fabula.

### Ask

- What follows from what?
- What is each actor trying to do at this point?
- What institution must notice this?
- What ordinary-life routine is now disturbed?
- What body, law, route, debt, or taboo blocks the next move?
- What false interpretation currently dominates?
- What clock is tightening?

### Rule

No major turn should exist solely because “the plot needs it.”

It must come from:
- pressure
- choice
- misreading
- structural response
- previously planted condition

---

## Phase 2: Build the milestone architecture

Convert the fabula into structural milestones.

### 1. Opening Pressure Signal
Purpose:
Show a world-specific tension already active before the major break.

Must reveal:
- ordinary life under pressure
- the specific texture of the world
- what “normal” currently costs

### 2. Introduction / Loaded Normality
Purpose:
Establish the protagonist inside material, institutional, and social constraints.

Must show:
- what they do all day
- what they cannot access
- what they owe
- what they fear
- what they misread
- what part of the world they naturally reveal

### 3. Inciting Breach
Purpose:
The first eventful rupture.

It must:
- break a local norm
- introduce real consequence
- force reinterpretation of normality

### 4. First Misreading / Refusal / Defensive Interpretation
Purpose:
Show how the protagonist’s knowledge world fails.

Typical forms:
- denial
- mistaken diagnosis
- trying to solve the problem with old assumptions
- obeying the wrong authority
- trusting the wrong text
- preserving the wrong loyalty

### 5. First Threshold / Lock-In
Purpose:
Cross the point where the protagonist cannot simply return to prior normality.

Typical lock-ins:
- public accusation
- legal entanglement
- debt taken on
- taboo broken
- evidence stolen
- route committed to
- witness silenced
- contamination risk accepted
- alliance publicly chosen

### 6. Consequence Wave I
Purpose:
Show that the world reacts.

This stage should produce:
- practical difficulties
- institutional attention
- bodily cost
- social rumor
- one widened stake beyond the protagonist

### 7. First Turning Point
Purpose:
A major reversal, reveal, or commitment that changes the direction of effort.

A good first turning point:
- invalidates the first plan
- reveals a stronger opponent
- reclassifies the problem
- turns private trouble into public trouble
- shows that the artifact, law, or witness meant something else

### 8. Midpoint Reframe
Purpose:
The story’s central re-understanding.

This should:
- convert the protagonist from reactive to more active
- reveal deeper structure
- sharpen the dramatic question
- increase cost of failure
- force a new strategy

At the midpoint, one of these should happen:
- false belief collapses
- hidden relation becomes visible
- artifact meaning flips
- institution’s real interest is exposed
- private and public stakes fuse

### 9. Consequence Wave II / Constriction
Purpose:
Close exits.

This stage should intensify:
- law
- social penalty
- bodily danger
- time pressure
- route scarcity
- betrayal risk
- internal division

### 10. Crisis
Purpose:
The impossible choice.

This is where:
- desire world and obligation world collide
- two values cannot both be preserved
- the protagonist must decide what story they are actually in

Good crisis forms:
- save person vs save truth
- preserve office vs prevent disaster
- obey taboo vs survive
- expose institution vs lose kin
- keep secret vs allow wider harm
- accept contamination vs lose chance forever

### 11. Climax / Decisive Turning Point
Purpose:
The irreversible act.

This is the moment after which the prior equilibrium cannot be restored.

The climax should:
- answer the dramatic question
- prove or defeat the premise
- actualize one future path and destroy another
- reveal what the protagonist has become willing to do or refuse

### 12. Fallout / Propagation
Purpose:
Show consequences spreading outward.

Must include at least two domains beyond interior feeling:
- institution
- kinship
- economy
- ritual order
- local rumor
- law
- public memory
- bodily aftermath
- geography of movement

### 13. Resolution / New Equilibrium
Purpose:
Show what is now stable, unstable, gained, lost, and misunderstood.

The resolution should state:
- what changed materially
- what changed relationally
- what changed epistemically
- what remains dangerous
- what remains hidden

### 14. Residue / Storyworld Afterimage
Optional but high-value.

Create one durable residue:
- a new proverb
- a revised decree
- a censored chronicle
- a forged letter left in archives
- an altered burial custom
- a stained route
- a false public memory
- a diegetic book written afterward

This is where the story gives the world something back.

## Phase 3: Build the relationship and opposition architecture

Do not leave interpersonal logic vague.

For each major pair, define:

- starting relation
- source of trust or distrust
- material dependence
- ideological mismatch
- what each misreads about the other
- what event changes the relation
- final relation status

### Required Pairs

- protagonist / primary opponent
- protagonist / helper
- protagonist / institution
- protagonist / intimate or kin figure
- protagonist / artifact-bearing witness or text tradition

### Rule

Opposition should not be reducible to villain intent.

At least one major opponent should be:
- structurally correct but morally dangerous
or
- morally understandable but structurally destructive

---

## Phase 4: Build the artifact integration plan

Use diegetic artifacts as narrative machinery, not decoration.

### Artifact Functions Inside Stories

- **Clue:** gives partial truth
- **Trap:** leads to false interpretation
- **Authority Mask:** grants false legitimacy
- **Inheritance Object:** transfers obligation
- **Ritual Tool:** operationally changes what can happen
- **Witness Fragment:** preserves missing past
- **Propaganda Engine:** shapes public reaction
- **Confession Device:** changes moral reading
- **Frame Device:** creates layered narration
- **Aftermath Object:** becomes residue

### Artifact Integration Questions

- Who authored this artifact and why?
- What can it know?
- What does it get wrong?
- Who treats it as authoritative?
- What happens if it is read correctly?
- What happens if it is suppressed?
- Does it touch protected mystery?
- Does it accidentally create unsupported canon?

### Rule

An artifact should never function as a lore dump.

It must alter:
- choice
- misunderstanding
- authority
- risk
- consequence

## Phase 5: Build the consequence map

Before scene handoff, force outward propagation.

### Consequence Layers

#### First-Order
Immediate consequences:
- injury
- debt
- accusation
- discovery
- contamination
- journey begun
- rule broken

#### Second-Order
Social and institutional responses:
- inquiry
- ban
- confiscation
- quarantine
- marriage pressure
- faction move
- patrols
- doctrinal reaction
- market opportunism

#### Third-Order
Equilibrium changes:
- altered route value
- changed burial practice
- new legal loophole
- sect split
- class shift
- species tension
- archive revision
- public myth formation

### Rule

A story outline is incomplete until it can answer:

“What now follows in the wider world?”

---

## Phase 6: Run canon and continuity gates

No architecture should be accepted before these checks.

### Canon Safety Check
Verify:
- no unsupported power appears
- no artifact knows what its author cannot know
- no public knowledge is public before it plausibly would be
- no protected mystery is accidentally resolved
- no local practice is universalized by accident
- no rejected canon is quietly used

### Story-to-Canon Classification
Tag every major story fact as:
- hard canon
- derived canon
- soft canon / local truth
- contested canon
- temporary story event
- mystery-adjacent but unresolved

### If the story requires new canon
Route those facts through:
- proposal generation
- canon adjudication
- downstream world updates

### Continuity Audit Questions
Before locking architecture, ask:
- what changed that should now appear elsewhere?
- what institution failed to respond?
- what capability now looks consequence-free?
- what local event is being accidentally treated as global?
- what diegetic texts need reframing as biased or partial?

## Acceptance Tests

This pipeline succeeds only if all of these are true.

### Story-Specificity Tests
- remove the world’s key institutions and the architecture collapses
- remove the world’s key embodiment rules and the architecture changes materially
- remove the world’s history and the architecture loses motive or structure
- remove the artifacts and at least one turning point disappears

### Causality Tests
- each major milestone follows from prior conditions
- no turning point is pure coincidence
- climax resolves the central dramatic question
- fallout visibly alters more than emotion

### Character Tests
- the protagonist begins inside real obligations
- the protagonist is wrong about something important
- at least one major decision costs them a valued identity, relationship, or safety
- the opponent pressure is not merely personal malice

### World Tests
- ordinary life matters
- institutions matter
- geography matters
- time matters
- mystery remains bounded, not dissolved

### Canon Tests
- no stealth canon creation
- no contradiction with invariants
- no diegetic leakage
- no local/global drift

## Where This Pipeline Stops

This pipeline stops when the story has:
- a chronological fabula
- milestone architecture
- relationship and opposition structure
- artifact integration plan
- consequence map
- canon and continuity gate results
- scene-handoff prep package

It does not yet create per-scene handoff cards.

---

## Final Rule

An outline is not complete because it has an opening, midpoint, and climax.

At the repository level, it is only ready when it also has:
- a kernel
- a world-specificity proof
- a pressure web
- an actant map
- a premise
- a designing principle
- a spine
- a milestone architecture
- a consequence map
- a scene handoff bundle

This document governs the architecture layer of that chain.
