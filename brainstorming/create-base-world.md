# Creating a base world from user's notions

## Purpose

Transform a user premise into a basic but robustly self-consistent story world.

This pipeline creates:
- the initial world kernel
- ontological and thematic invariants
- first-pass geography, peoples, institutions, resources, history, and daily life
- pressure systems and mystery reserves
- a world state ready for later canon additions

This pipeline is for world establishment, not for plot generation.

---

## Inputs

Required:
- user premise
- genre
- exclusions / dislikes
- target mood or tone
- desired degree of realism vs mythic abstraction
- intended use case:
  - novel
  - RPG setting
  - open-world game
  - anthology world
  - transmedia setting
  - lore sandbox

Optional:
- inspirations
- anti-inspirations
- scale of intended stories
- preferred density of lore
- desired mystery level
- audience age band
- ethical red lines
- interest in cultural complexity vs pulp simplicity

---

## Output Bundle

This pipeline must produce:

- `WORLD_KERNEL.md`
- `INVARIANTS.md`
- `ONTOLOGY.md`

## Phase 0: Normalize the User Premise

Convert the user's idea into a precise design brief.

### Required Extraction

From the user's wording, extract:
- genre identity
- implied baseline reality
- intended ontological departures
- power fantasy level
- social density
- violence level
- cosmological scale
- likely focal conflicts
- what the user finds exciting
- what must not happen

### Example

User input:
"I want a low-magic, sword & sorcery fantasy world populated by humans and many sentient animal humanoids."

Extracted brief:
- low-magic lived experience
- sword & sorcery, not epic destiny fantasy
- multiple sentient species are ordinary world structure, not rare curiosities
- likely emphasis on frontier contact, prejudice, trade, ecology, bodily difference, and uneven civilization
- magic must not trivialize ordinary danger
- civilization probably fragmented rather than totalizing

---

## Phase 1: Establish Baseline via Minimal Departure

Assume baseline reality unless explicitly changed.

For each domain, mark:

- unchanged from reality
- altered
- unknown

Domains:
- physics
- cosmology
- biology
- reproduction
- lifespan
- sentience
- language
- religion
- governance
- warfare
- medicine
- metallurgy
- agriculture
- labor
- family structure
- sexuality and kinship
- trade
- literacy
- death
- afterlife
- weather
- disease
- ecology

Important:
Do not begin by inventing differences.
Begin by locating exactly where difference enters.

---

## Phase 2: Draft the World Kernel

Write a one-paragraph version first, then expand.

### Required Fields

- genre contract
- tonal contract
- chronotope
- primary world difference
- major pressures
- natural story engines
- what counts as ordinary life
- what counts as wonder, terror, or taboo

### Rule

The world kernel must distinguish:
- what is common
- what is rare
- what is impossible

If the world cannot answer that, it is not ready.

---

## Phase 3: Build Invariants

Define non-negotiable truths.

At minimum, create invariants for:

### Ontology
- what beings exist
- what cannot exist
- what counts as death
- whether souls exist
- whether spirits, gods, monsters, curses, or miracles are real

### Causality
- what every power costs
- whether fate exists
- whether prophecy can be trusted
- whether corruption spreads materially, spiritually, socially, memetically, or all of the above

### Distribution
- who can access violence, literacy, mobility, magic, ritual authority, law, trade, land

### Social Order
- where legitimacy comes from
- what kinship does politically
- what labor structures the world
- what taboos hold the social fabric together

### Aesthetic Identity
- what must remain perceptually true for the world to feel like itself

---

## Phase 4: Define the Chronotope

Do not treat setting as map only.

Define:
- travel speeds
- communication speeds
- seasonal rhythms
- ritual calendars
- war season / harvest season / migration season
- urban vs rural temporalities
- ruin-depth and historical layering
- how ancient the past feels in ordinary life

Questions:
- Is this a frontier world, decaying empire world, archipelago world, road world, river world, pilgrimage world, ruin world?
- What does distance mean emotionally and politically?
- What kinds of encounters are normal because of the world's time-space structure?

---

## Phase 5: Define Ontological Systems

Model the world's deep rule systems.

### Sentience and Species
If multiple sentient species exist, specify for each:
- body plan
- diet
- movement capabilities
- senses
- vulnerability profile
- reproductive pattern
- lifespan
- social density
- relation to clothing, tools, architecture, and warfare
- relation to taboo, religion, and law
- relation to environment

Rule:
Species cannot be cosmetic reskins.
Embodiment must matter.

### Magic / Supernatural / Technology
For each system define:
- source
- access path
- training
- cost
- reliability
- failure states
- material requirements
- social perception
- policing / regulation / taboo
- mundane substitutes
- visible signs
- long-term consequences

Rule:
A "low-magic world" must define low-magic in operational terms:
- rare practitioners?
- weak effects?
- high cost?
- secrecy?
- low public visibility?
- magic as relic rather than skill?
- magic powerful but civilization-poor in relation to it?

---

## Phase 6: Build Geography and Ecology

Define:
- terrain zones
- climate bands
- choke points
- trade corridors
- migration routes
- predator zones
- disease ecologies
- sacred or cursed terrain
- extractive zones
- food baselines
- settlement limits

Rule:
No polity, species distribution, or trade pattern may be invented before geography and ecology make it plausible.

Questions:
- What does each region produce?
- What cannot be grown or mined?
- Which species are advantaged or disadvantaged by the terrain?
- What structures architecture and clothing?
- What terrain breeds raiding, isolation, bureaucracy, nomadism, piracy, monasticism, or road empires?

---

## Phase 7: Build Institutions

For each institution, define:
- function
- legitimacy source
- material base
- violence capacity
- recruitment
- succession
- internal contradictions
- relation to literacy, religion, economy, and war

Mandatory institutional domains:
- family / clan / household
- law / custom / judgment
- religion / ritual authority
- landholding / resource control
- military / defense
- trade / guilds / caravans / shipping
- healing / medicine
- burial / death management
- education / apprenticeship
- recordkeeping / archives / myth transmission

Rule:
A world with no institutional thinking is not a world.
It is a sketch.

---

## Phase 8: Build Everyday Life

This phase is mandatory.

For each major region / class / species cluster, define:
- food
- water
- sanitation
- housing
- clothing
- heating / cooling
- childbirth
- childrearing
- aging
- courtship / mating / marriage / pair bonding or alternatives
- labor rhythm
- leisure
- intoxication
- prayer / ritual
- cleanliness / impurity concepts
- common injuries
- medicine access
- disposal of the dead
- oral storytelling / songs / sayings
- common fears
- common aspirations

Rule:
If the world is only designed from the perspective of rulers, adventurers, and cosmology, it will feel fake.

---

## Phase 9: Build History as Pressure, Not Decoration

History must explain current structure.

Create at least:
- deep past
- remembered past
- recent past
- current unstable present

For each major historical event, define:
- what changed materially
- what changed institutionally
- what changed symbolically
- who benefited
- who still tells the story
- what scars remain in architecture, law, prejudice, ritual, landscape, or economy

Rule:
Past events must leave residues.
No history should vanish without a trace.

---

## Phase 10: Cross-Domain Consistency Passes

Run these passes in order.

### Ecology Pass
Do species, food chains, settlement density, monsters, and resources coexist plausibly?

### Economy Pass
What is scarce?
Who moves goods?
What stores value?
Who eats badly?
What creates wealth?
What breaks trade?

### Political Pass
Who can tax?
Who can coordinate violence?
Who is recognized as legitimate?
What are the boundaries between formal power and real power?

### Warfare Pass
Who fights, how, with what logistics?
How do species differences matter?
How do terrain and season matter?
How does magic or relic technology alter force projection?

### Religion Pass
What is sacred?
Who interprets the sacred?
How do ritual and legitimacy interact?
What happens at birth, oath, contamination, marriage, death?

### Language / Communication Pass
What languages, dialects, scripts, trade tongues, ritual languages, or taboo names exist?
How does species embodiment affect speech or writing?

### Daily-Life Pass
Would an ordinary person recognize this as a livable world rather than a concept art portfolio?

---

## Phase 11: Equilibrium Explanation

This is one of the most important phases.

Explain why the world has not already shifted into a more obvious nearby arrangement.

Examples:
- If relic magic is powerful, why has it not centralized empires?
- If many sentient species coexist, why has one not dominated all biomes?
- If corruption artifacts exist, why is civilization not already gone?
- If mercenary bands thrive, why do states tolerate them?
- If literacy is rare, how is memory stabilized?

You must produce:
- stabilizers
- bottlenecks
- taboos
- monopolies
- ecological checks
- ideological checks
- infrastructural limits
- failure costs

Rule:
Every extraordinary capability must meet resistance from reality.

---

## Phase 12: Generate Pressure Systems and Story Engines

A world should not merely be stable.
It should be fertile.

Generate pressures under:
- class
- species coexistence
- law vs custom
- scarcity
- ritual pollution
- migration
- memory of past catastrophe
- ruins / relic extraction
- contested borders
- splinter faiths
- succession
- trade asymmetry
- ecological collapse
- military transition
- disease
- forbidden knowledge

Then identify story engines:
- what naturally produces conflict
- what naturally produces journeys
- what naturally produces betrayal
- what naturally produces intimacy across divisions
- what naturally produces myths and lies

---

## Phase 13: Install Mystery Reserve

Do not explain everything.

Create three categories:
- active mysteries that characters care about
- passive depths that enrich atmosphere
- forbidden mysteries that should remain unresolved for a long time

For each mystery:
- known facts
- unknown facts
- common in-world interpretations
- disallowed cheap answers
- domains touched
- whether it is safe for future canon resolution

Rule:
Mystery must be bounded, not mushy.

---

## Phase 14: Draft Initial Canon Ledger

Record all accepted baseline facts with IDs.

Every fact must specify:
- scope
- status
- source
- consequences
- affected files

Nothing stays as "we'll remember that."

---

## Phase 15: Validation and Rejection Tests

Reject or revise the world if any of the following are true:

- species are cosmetic
- magic has no cost, limit, or social consequence
- institutions do not explain how power persists
- geography has no effect on culture or politics
- history leaves no residue
- daily life is absent
- every mystery is actually just vagueness
- world tone and ontology are mismatched
- the world supports only heroes, not populations
- there is no explanation for why extraordinary facts have not transformed everything

---

## Final Deliverables

The pipeline is complete only when it outputs:

1. one-paragraph world kernel
2. one-page invariant summary
3. one-page everyday-life summary
4. species / peoples table
5. geography-pressure map summary
6. institutional summary
7. historical pressure summary
8. pressure systems list
9. mystery reserve list
10. canon ledger entries

## Model Roles for LLM Use

Run separate agents or passes:

- Ontology Architect
- Geography/Ecology Analyst
- Institutions Analyst
- Everyday Life Analyst
- Continuity Critic
- Theme/Tone Critic
- Pressure-System Designer
- Mystery Curator

Then run a synthesis pass.

Do not use one monolithic prompt if avoidable.

---

## Definition of Success

The world succeeds if:
- it can generate many stories
- it can survive further canon additions
- ordinary life feels implied even when not shown
- extraordinary elements do not float free of consequences
- mystery remains without incoherence
- later authors can add content without guessing at the world's logic
