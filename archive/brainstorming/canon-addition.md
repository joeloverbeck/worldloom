# Canon addition, review and adjudication

## Purpose

Evaluate a proposed new canon fact against the existing story world.

This pipeline does not ask:
"Is this cool?"

It asks:
- What is this actually changing?
- What must now also be true?
- What breaks?
- What needs revision?
- Should this be accepted at all?
- If accepted, at what scope and under what limits?

---

## Input

Required:
- proposed canon fact
- current World Kernel
- current Invariants
- all canon facts in affected domains
- unresolved contradiction list
- relevant Mystery Reserve entries

Optional:
- user intention for the new fact
- desired narrative effect
- desired rarity
- preferred scope
- whether user is willing to revise existing lore

---

## Output

One of:
- ACCEPT
- ACCEPT_WITH_REQUIRED_UPDATES
- ACCEPT_AS_LOCAL_EXCEPTION
- ACCEPT_AS_CONTESTED_BELIEF
- REVISE_AND_RESUBMIT
- REJECT

And:
- consequence map
- contradiction map
- required file updates
- repair options
- burden assessment
- story-yield assessment

---

## Phase 0: Normalize the Proposal

Convert the proposal into operational form.

### Extract

- statement
- type
- intended dramatic purpose
- novelty level
- implied scope
- implied rarity
- implied access pattern
- implied consequences
- user-stated constraints

### Canon Fact Types

Classify the proposal as one or more of:
- ontological rule
- capability
- artifact
- species trait
- institution
- ritual
- law
- historical event
- social practice
- taboo
- technology
- metaphysical claim
- resource distribution
- hidden truth
- local anomaly
- contested belief

Rule:
Never evaluate a surface sentence only.
Always identify the underlying world-change.

Example:
"Magic artifacts are sometimes found buried, and they can cause corruption and wildly different and dangerous effects."

Underlying changes:
- buried relic emergence exists
- relics remain active across time
- contact risks corruption
- effects are variable and difficult to predict
- relic discovery is a repeating phenomenon
- there may be treasure economies, ritual quarantines, black markets, cults, contamination zones, salvage specialists, and political struggles over containment

---

## Phase 1: Scope Detection

Determine actual scope, not just stated scope.

For each proposal, define:
- geographic scope
- temporal scope
- social scope
- visibility
- reproducibility
- institutional awareness
- secrecy level
- diffusion risk

Important:
Many user proposals appear local but logically tend toward larger scope.
This phase catches that.

---

## Phase 2: Invariant Check

Test the proposal against all relevant invariants.

### Outcomes

- compatible
- compatible only if scoped locally
- compatible only if reclassified as belief rather than truth
- compatible only if an invariant is revised
- incompatible

### Hard Rejection Triggers

Reject unless user explicitly wants a world rewrite:
- direct violation of non-negotiable ontology
- collapse of primary genre contract
- contradiction of world-defining scarcity/distribution logic
- world-scale destabilization with no plausible stabilizers
- retroactive invalidation of too much established canon

---

## Phase 3: Underlying Capability / Constraint Analysis

Determine what power, permission, vulnerability, or change the fact introduces.

Ask:
- What can now be done that could not previously be done?
- What can no longer be safely assumed?
- What becomes easier?
- What becomes harder?
- What becomes more valuable?
- What becomes more feared?
- What becomes politically useful?

Examples:
- reprogramming robots -> labor and military automation implications
- buried corrupting artifacts -> archaeology, grave economies, cult activity, contamination law, ruin quarantine, relic smuggling
- animal-humanoids with gliding ability -> architecture, warfare, courier systems, child safety, prestige, hunting, verticality of cities

---

## Phase 4: Prerequisites and Bottlenecks

List everything required for the proposal to manifest.

Check:
- knowledge
- training
- tools
- infrastructure
- bodily requirements
- environmental conditions
- ritual conditions
- raw materials
- social permission
- legal permission
- secrecy
- time investment
- maintenance
- recovery after failure

Then classify each requirement:
- common
- uncommon
- rare
- monopolized
- forbidden
- extinct
- region-locked

This phase often converts a world-breaking proposal into a viable limited one.

---

## Phase 5: Diffusion and Copycat Analysis

This is the phase most weak lore misses.

Ask:
- If this is possible, who else should also be attempting it?
- Which factions would suppress it?
- Which institutions would monopolize it?
- Which black markets would form around it?
- Which ordinary people would adapt around it?
- Why has it not already diffused everywhere?

### Required Outputs

- primary adopters
- secondary adopters
- suppressors
- skeptics
- profiteers
- victims
- non-adopters and why not

Rule:
If a capability is useful and reproducible, diffusion pressure must be assumed.

---

## Phase 6: Consequence Propagation

Run at least three layers of propagation.

### First-Order Consequences
Immediate direct effects.

Examples:
- artifacts cause corruption
- relic hunters emerge
- ritual specialists are needed
- contaminated ruins become feared

### Second-Order Consequences
Institutional and social responses.

Examples:
- new quarantine laws
- relic licensing
- grave robbery panic
- anti-corruption sects
- smuggler guilds
- regional cleansing rites
- warlords weaponizing relic contamination

### Third-Order Consequences
World equilibrium shifts.

Examples:
- border regions become depopulated
- aristocracies monopolize relic screening
- distrust of burial grounds changes funerary practice
- pilgrimage sites turn into contamination economies
- treasure-hunting reshapes class mobility
- folklore becomes practical hazard guidance

Required domains:
- everyday life
- economy
- law
- religion
- warfare
- status order
- kinship
- architecture
- mobility
- environment
- taboo / pollution concepts
- language / slang
- memory / myth

---

## Phase 7: Counterfactual Pressure Test

Ask the brutal question:

"If this were true, why does the existing world not look more different already?"

Then answer with explicit limiting conditions.

Typical stabilizers:
- rarity
- secrecy
- high mortality
- unreliability
- expensive prerequisites
- monopoly control
- taboo
- hard-to-transport materials
- activation conditions
- geographic isolation
- incompatibility with ordinary labor systems
- short lifespan of effects
- self-destructive side effects
- elite suppression
- recent discovery
- mistaken public understanding

Rule:
Do not hand-wave with "people just don't use it much."
State why.

---

## Phase 8: Contradiction Classification

Classify detected conflicts.

### Hard Contradiction
Cannot coexist with current canon without changing established truths.

### Soft Contradiction
Could coexist, but existing canon now owes explanation or visible consequences.

### Latent Burden
Not a contradiction yet, but the proposal creates mandatory future lore work.

### Scope Drift Risk
The proposal is acceptable only if kept local, temporary, or secret.

### Tone/Thematic Mismatch
The proposal does not break logic but makes the world feel unlike itself.

---

## Phase 9: Repair Pass

If the proposal is promising but destabilizing, propose repairs.

Repair strategies:
- reduce scope
- reduce reproducibility
- add cost
- add side effects
- add bottlenecks
- localize geographically
- localize temporally
- make it recent
- make it heritable to a narrow group
- make it taboo
- shift it from objective truth to contested belief
- split it into multiple narrower facts
- move it into Mystery Reserve rather than open canon

Important:
Repairs must preserve the user's intended dramatic benefit where possible.

---

## Phase 10: Narrative and Thematic Fit

Evaluate:
- Does this deepen the world's identity?
- Does it generate good tensions?
- Does it trivialize existing struggle?
- Does it universalize specialness?
- Does it undermine mystery by explaining too much?
- Does it enrich ordinary life, or only exceptional scenes?
- Does it create story engines or merely clutter?

Reject facts that are technically consistent but dramatically flattening.

---

## Phase 11: Adjudication

### ACCEPT
Use only if:
- invariant-safe
- consequences manageable
- scope clear
- burden acceptable
- world identity strengthened

### ACCEPT_WITH_REQUIRED_UPDATES
Use if:
- good addition
- but multiple world files must be changed immediately

### ACCEPT_AS_LOCAL_EXCEPTION
Use if:
- globally destabilizing
- but valuable as a regional anomaly, cult practice, hidden order, one-time historical incident, or bounded technology

### ACCEPT_AS_CONTESTED_BELIEF
Use if:
- valuable for atmosphere, ideology, mystery, politics, or diegetic texture
- but not wanted as objective world truth

### REVISE_AND_RESUBMIT
Use if:
- promising
- but underspecified in scope, cost, or consequences

### REJECT
Use if:
- breaks invariants
- destroys genre contract
- creates implausible systemic omissions
- weakens world identity
- imposes too much retcon burden for too little gain

---

## Phase 12: Required Update List

If not rejected, produce mandatory updates.

For each affected file, specify:
- what must be added
- what must be revised
- what new questions arise
- what ordinary-life consequences must now be visible

No canon addition is complete until these updates are made.

## Output Schema

```yaml
proposal_id: PA-0012
proposal_statement: >
  Magic artifacts are sometimes found buried and can cause corruption
  and wildly different dangerous effects.
classification:
  type:
    - artifact
    - hazard
    - metaphysical_effect
scope:
  recommended: regional
  public_visibility: uneven
  diffusion_risk: high
invariant_check:
  result: compatible_with_repairs
underlying_changes:
  - recurring relic emergence
  - contamination risk from excavation/contact
  - relic economies and containment practices
prerequisites:
  - ancient burial sites
  - survivable relic persistence
  - discoverability by scavengers/miners/grave robbers
bottlenecks:
  - rarity
  - poor detectability
  - high mortality
  - ritual containment knowledge
consequences:
  first_order:
    - relic sickness
    - salvage cults
    - fear of disturbed graves
  second_order:
    - quarantine law
    - licensed relic wardens
    - anti-smuggling patrols
  third_order:
    - funerary reform
    - border contamination zones
    - prestige economy around safe identification
contradictions:
  hard: []
  soft:
    - prior ruins had too little containment infrastructure
repair_options:
  - make active artifacts rare and clustered in specific geologies
  - require disturbance plus blood contact
  - effects decay after exposure
adjudication: accept_with_required_updates
required_updates:
  - MAGIC_OR_TECH_SYSTEMS.md
  - INSTITUTIONS.md
  - EVERYDAY_LIFE.md
  - TIMELINE.md
  - OPEN_QUESTIONS.md
story_yield: high
burden: medium_high
```

## Mandatory LLM Roles

Run the proposal through at least these critics:

- Continuity Archivist
- Systems/Economy Critic
- Politics/Institution Critic
- Everyday-Life Critic
- Theme/Tone Critic
- Mystery Curator

Then synthesize.

---

## Final Rule

No canon fact should ever be accepted as a sentence only.

It must be accepted as:
- a sentence
- a scope
- a consequence web
- an update burden
- a place in the world's ontology
