# **The master pattern**

Your favorite worlds are **not primarily places**. They are **rule-propagation systems**.

A strong world, by your taste, usually has this structure:

**One impossible or extreme condition alters survival.**  
 **Survival alters institutions.**  
 **Institutions alter bodies, professions, rituals, laws, architecture, factions, myths, and stories.**  
 **The present misunderstands the origin of the condition.**  
 **The best stories are about discovering, exploiting, resisting, or being crushed by that misunderstanding.**

That is the core grammar underneath almost every example you listed.

The worldbuilding you like most is not “cool stuff exists.” It is:

**Cool stuff exists because the world has been forced to adapt to a weird foundational wound.**

---

# **1\. The “impossible fact” pattern**

Almost every world you like begins with a single impossible, catastrophic, or absurd fact that everything else must obey.

Examples:

* **The Matrix:** reality is a simulation used to control humans.  
* **Made in Abyss:** descent is easy, ascent is cursed.  
* **Attack on Titan:** humanity lives inside walls because Titans roam outside.  
* **Dune:** one desert planet produces the resource that makes interstellar civilization possible.  
* **Frostpunk:** civilization is organized around heat.  
* **Mortal Engines:** cities move, hunt, and eat other cities.  
* **His Dark Materials:** your soul exists outside your body as a daemon.  
* **The City & the City:** people are legally trained to ignore another city occupying the same space.  
* **Blame\!:** civilization’s infrastructure no longer recognizes humanity as authorized.  
* **Parallel Paradise:** women die around twenty unless a strange sexual/reproductive exception occurs.  
* **Bloodborne:** healing blood is also corruption.  
* **Dishonored:** industrial civilization is powered by whale oil tied to occult horror.

The pattern is:

**A great world can often be reduced to one impossible sentence.**

But the key is that the sentence must be fertile. It must generate consequences in every direction.

Weak version:

“There are dragons.”

Strong version:

“Dragons are the only creatures whose bones can anchor cities against earthquakes, so every kingdom is built on dragon graveyards, and dragon poaching is both sacrilege and urban planning.”

Your taste strongly favors the second kind.

# **2\. The “consequence engine” pattern**

You like settings where the premise propagates aggressively.

A new fact should affect:

* economy  
* law  
* food  
* medicine  
* sex  
* family structure  
* class  
* war  
* architecture  
* professions  
* childhood  
* religion  
* crime  
* propaganda  
* local language  
* mourning customs  
* infrastructure  
* social prejudice  
* historical memory

This is why **Pathologic**, **Broken Earth**, **Fallout**, **Dune**, **Frostpunk**, and **Parallel Paradise** matter so much in your list. A fact never just sits there.

For example:

“Women die at twenty” is not just a tragic curse.  
 It changes adulthood, inheritance, village leadership, mourning, fertility, education, religion, time horizons, sexuality, records, and settlement continuity.

Or:

“Heat is scarce” is not just weather.  
 It becomes law, morality, class geography, labor policy, architecture, and political legitimacy.

The deeper rule:

**A good canon fact should create second-order, third-order, and fourth-order consequences.**

Your app should probably treat every canon addition as a propagation problem.

# **3\. Survival constraints are your favorite source of institutions**

A repeated pattern: the world has a physical or metaphysical survival constraint, and society has built institutions around it.

Examples:

* **Frostpunk:** cold creates the Generator, heat zones, emergency law, child labor debates, faith/order politics.  
* **Dune:** water scarcity creates Fremen customs, stillsuits, water discipline, death rites, ecological patience.  
* **Made in Abyss:** the Curse creates delvers, whistle ranks, orphan training, relic economies, descent taboos.  
* **The Expanse:** oxygen, gravity, distance, and water create Belter class biology and politics.  
* **Broken Earth:** recurrent apocalypse creates comms, castes, storecaches, survival law, reproductive control.  
* **Attack on Titan:** Titans create Walls, military branches, ODM gear, spatial class structure, Survey Corps martyrdom.  
* **Nausicaä:** toxic spores create masks, wind-dependent settlements, ecological taboos, new myths.  
* **Blades in the Dark:** ghosts and broken death create electroplasm, lightning barriers, spirit wardens, occult crime.

The pattern:

**Institutions should not be generic. They should exist because the world would kill people without them.**

A weak institution:

“There is a wizard academy.”

A strong institution:

“There is a school that trains children to hear approaching earthquakes because only untrained children can detect the buried gods before they wake.”

**The best technology in your preferred worlds is compensatory technology.**

# **Condensed preference formula**

Your preferred worldbuilding can be summarized as:

**A strange rule creates a survival problem.**  
 **Survival produces institutions.**  
 **Institutions produce professions, taboos, technologies, factions, documents, and crimes.**  
 **The truth of the rule is historically buried or misinterpreted.**  
 **Different groups adapt differently.**  
 **The protagonist or story ruptures the adaptation.**  
 **Every new fact must ripple through the whole system.**

# **The strongest single design rule**

For your canon app, I would make this a core rule:

**No canon fact is allowed to remain local unless the world is deliberately trivial.**

Every new fact should trigger propagation questions:

* Who changes behavior because of this?  
* Which institution claims jurisdiction?  
* Which faction lies about it?  
* Which profession profits?  
* Which taboo is threatened?  
* Which old document seems newly relevant?  
* Which settlement is destabilized?  
* Which technology or ritual compensates?  
* Which group becomes more feared, needed, or exploitable?  
* What story can now happen that could not happen before?

That is the deep pattern across your list.

Your favorite worlds are worlds where canon behaves like pressure in a sealed system. Add one fact, and the whole structure shifts.
